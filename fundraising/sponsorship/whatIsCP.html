What is a Coporate Sponsorship?
Even though sponsorship is often associated with the non-profit sector it should not be confused with philanthropy. Instead of thinking of a sponsorship as a donation it should be regarded more as a business transaction where the sponsor gets something in return from their sponsorship. A sponsorship can be defined as:
"A cash and/or in-kind fee paid to a property in return for access to the exploitable commercial potential associated with that property" (IEG, 2017).
An in-kind gift is not cash but the donation of goods, services or time (MissionBox, 2018). In-kind sponsors are also referred to as trade sponsors or soft dollars. Free food, printing, seminars and radio advertising are all examples of in-kind gifts. A property is the entity available for sponsorship. I can be a gala, event, entertainment, sports team, a person or other causes where a company is partnering up with an NGO. Sometimes sponsored NGOs receive both in-kind and cash gifts rather than being a choice between one or the other.
The pivotal element in a sponsorship is that the sponsor can achieve commercial objectives through the property provided by the NGO. In other words, sponsors give NGOs money and resources to connect them with people who buy things (Hollander, 2014). Sponsorship makes it possible for a company to stay front of mind with their target demographic by connection an emotion to the positive experience of an event create by an NGO. In this sense company get access to their market and a NGO's volunteers, board members, donors and clients.
Because a sponsorship can be defined as a relationship it is something that doesn't develop overnight but takes some time to cultivate. This means that a gift certificate or free food is not considered a sponsorship but a donation.
